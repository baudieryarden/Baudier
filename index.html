Overview

My Yard — Layout & Microclimate

New Orleans Zone 9b backyard with a mature pecan canopy, large banana grove on the west side, two galvanized raised beds connected by a cow panel arch trellis, a small greenhouse, and a perennial border bed by the shed. The beds sit in the best sun corridor in the yard — open southern sky with roughly 6–7 hours of direct sun daily. Shade arrives from the northeast around 5pm.

Food Forest Layers Already in Place
Canopy (pecan) → Sub-canopy (banana, fig) → Shrub (border perennials) → Ground (raised beds) → Vine (arch trellis crops). Missing layers to add: understory fruiting shrubs (elderberry, pawpaw) and fungal layer (wine cap mushrooms at banana base).

Large grove on west side. Creates warm humid microclimate, acts as windbreak. Leaves are high-potassium compost tea gold. Plant turmeric and ginger at the base.

Small greenhouse to the right of the beds. Use for seed starting December–February. Start tomatoes in December for February transplants. Perfect for lisianthus and snapdragons.

Growing Spaces

The Raised Beds — What's Growing

Oval/elongated galvanized bed. Currently home to the flower garden and herb collection with the left side of the arch trellis for tall climbers.

Plant compactly with low-growing companions acting as living mulch to suppress weeds, retain moisture, and feed soil biology.

Fennel Warning — Remove from Beds
Fennel releases allelopathic chemicals that inhibit tomatoes, beans, lisianthus, and most vegetables. Move fennel to its own container away from both beds. It will be happier isolated and so will everything else.

Permanent Plants

Trees, Fruit & Perennial Food Plants

Do NOT judge ripeness by color alone
— LSU light varieties can be ripe while still quite green. Look for ALL of these signs together: fruit droops downward on stem · soft to gentle squeeze · skin begins to wrinkle slightly · neck/stem withers and bends · tiny drop of nectar at eye (bottom) · pulls off with gentle upward twist. Check every morning once softening begins — they ripen in 2–3 days and birds watch just as closely.

Shed Bed — What's Growing

Brick-edged perennial border beside the shed. Features coral vine on the fence, established perennials, and the ongoing battle with cat's claw vine. A low-maintenance pollinator and sensory garden that feeds beneficial insects which support the vegetable beds nearby.

Cat's Claw Vine Battle Plan
Macfadyena unguis-cati is one of the most invasive vines in Louisiana. This week: cut ALL vines at the base — do NOT compost (bag and trash, they re-root). Dig out underground tubers (look like small potatoes) — these are the real enemy. Spray any regrowth immediately with vinegar + salt + dish soap. Expect a 2–3 year battle. Soil & Feeding

Fertilizer, Compost Tea & Soil Health

Your pecan tree has been building a mycorrhizal fungal network in your soil for years. Your raised beds are likely tapping into it. To protect and strengthen it: never use fungicides anywhere in the yard. Add mycorrhizal inoculant to transplant holes. Your no-dig, living mulch philosophy is already perfect. Plant native species around the tree base (goldenrod, spiderwort, wild bergamot) to strengthen the network.

Your Weed Compost Tea Bucket
Already brilliant — free, hyperlocal fertilizer perfectly tuned to your soil. Always dilute to the color of weak iced tea before applying. Apply to moist (not dry) soil, early morning. Every 7–10 days during growing season.
Comfrey (Symphytum officinale)
— Plant 1–2 in a corner of your yard. Perennial in 9b, comes back bigger every year. Harvest leaves 4–5 times per season. Makes the most potent homemade fertilizer tea of anything you can grow — extremely high in potassium and phosphorus. Known as "liquid gold." The one plant that could replace every store-bought fertilizer for your entire garden long-term.

Plant Identification

Weeds & Volunteers — Keep or Pull?

Fastest ID Method: The Smell Test
Crush a leaf and smell it. Lemon/citrus = lemon balm (keep). Strong mint = wild mint (keep). Herbal/catnip = catnip (keep). No real smell = likely chamber bitter or caesar weed (pull or use for tea). Milky sap = spurge (pull and wash hands).

Crop Guide

Tomatoes — Troubleshooting & Variety Guide

Why New Orleans Tomatoes Stall in Summer
Above 90°F daytime and 75°F nighttime, tomato pollen becomes sterile and fruit cannot ripen (can't produce lycopene above 95°F). This is normal. Your real tomato harvest windows are: March–June and September–November. Plants going in April are racing the clock — next year start in December in the greenhouse and transplant February.

Greenhouse & Seeds

Seed Starting Guide — Your 26 Varieties

All seed starting information for your personal seed collection. Your greenhouse is a major advantage — use it December–March for warm-season starts and September–October for cool-season transplant production.

New Orleans Timing Key Insight
Your "winter" is your "spring." All peppers are PERENNIAL in 9b — cut back in February for bigger year-2 plants. Start tomatoes and peppers in December in your greenhouse. Tomatoes going in before mid-February will outperform April plantings dramatically. Cucumbers started in late January will be producing by April.
Universal Tips for Your Greenhouse
Use quality seed starting mix (not garden soil). Bottom water trays to prevent damping off fungus. Keep seeds at 70–80°F for germination. Label everything immediately. Harden off transplants over 7–10 days. Ensure airflow inside greenhouse to prevent mold in Louisiana humidity. Succession sow greens and herbs every 3 weeks.
Zone 9b Perennial Advantage
These plants in your seed collection are perennial in New Orleans — cut them back in late winter instead of pulling. Year 2 plants are always dramatically more productive than year 1. This is one of the biggest advantages of Zone 9b gardening that most guides don't emphasize.
