MY YARD — LAYOUT & MICROCLIMATE

New Orleans Zone 9b backyard with a mature pecan canopy, large banana grove on the west side, two galvanized raised beds connected by a cow panel arch trellis, a small greenhouse, and a perennial border bed by the shed. The beds sit in the best sun corridor in the yard — open southern sky with roughly 6–7 hours of direct sun daily. Shade arrives from the northeast around 5pm.

🍉
CAL-SWEET WATERMELON
Citrullus lanatus
DIRECT SOW April 80–90 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow preferred in April when soil is 70°F+. Use biodegradable pots if starting indoors — taproots hate disturbance. Sow 2 seeds, thin to 1. Direct vine OUT of bed onto lawn to sprawl.
GROWING NOTES
Ripe when: tendril nearest fruit is brown and dry, ground spot turns yellow, hollow thud when tapped. Reduce water near harvest to concentrate sugars.
COMPANIONS
✦ Nasturtiums, marigolds, radishes
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Hollow heart: inconsistent watering. Poor sweetness: too much nitrogen. Vine wilts: squash vine borer — check stem base.
🍉
DIXIE QUEEN WATERMELON
Citrullus lanatus
DIRECT SOW April 85 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow April in warm soil, 3 seeds per hill, thin to 2. Space hills 6–8 ft apart. Southern heirloom with thick rind that resists splitting in Louisiana summer rains.
GROWING NOTES
Harvest same cues as Cal-Sweet. Don't grow both adjacent if saving seeds — they may cross-pollinate.
COMPANIONS
✦ Nasturtiums, borage, corn
✕ avoid: Fennel, potatoes
TROUBLESHOOT
Fruit splitting: inconsistent water — mulch heavily. No fruit set: need 2 hills for cross-pollination, or hand-pollinate with a paintbrush.
🥕
PURPLE CARROT
Daucus carota
DIRECT SOW Sept–Nov 70–80 days
HOW TO START
ALWAYS direct sow — carrots hate transplanting. Sow thinly, cover ¼ inch fine soil or vermiculite. Keep surface consistently moist every day for 2–3 weeks until germination. Cover with burlap to retain moisture.
GROWING NOTES
Purple color intensifies in cooler temperatures. Best harvest October–February after cool nights. Your raised beds are perfect — loose, deep, rock-free.
COMPANIONS
✦ Rosemary, sage, chives, leeks, onions
✕ avoid: Dill — stunts carrots
TROUBLESHOOT
No germination: surface dried out — the #1 failure point. Keep moist without fail. Forked roots: clods in soil — add fine compost on top.
🥬
SLOBOLT LOOSE LEAF LETTUCE
Lactuca sativa
DIRECT SOW Sept–Mar 45–50 days
HOW TO START
Surface sow — lettuce needs light to germinate, barely cover. Succession sow every 3 weeks September through March. Slobolt resists bolting 2–3 extra weeks vs regular varieties.
GROWING NOTES
Cut-and-come-again: harvest outer leaves only, never pull whole plant. December–February plants will be your best — cool temps, no bolt pressure.
COMPANIONS
✦ Chives, garlic, radishes, strawberries
✕ avoid: Fennel, celery
TROUBLESHOOT
Bitter leaves: heat stress or irregular watering. Bolting: temps above 75°F consistently — harvest immediately and resow in fall.
🌿
BOUQUET DILL
Anethum graveolens
DIRECT SOW Sept–Mar 40–60 days
HOW TO START
ALWAYS direct sow — taproot resents transplanting. Scatter seeds thinly, cover lightly. Self-seeds freely once established. Pair with your cucumbers for fresh pickles.
GROWING NOTES
Harvest foliage before flowers open for best flavor. Let some go to seed for self-seeding and pickling. Black Swallowtail caterpillars will eat it — let them, they become butterflies!
COMPANIONS
✦ Cabbage, lettuce, onion
✕ avoid: Carrots, tomatoes
TROUBLESHOOT
Leggy: not enough sun or too warm. Disappears: caterpillars ate it — resow immediately. Bolting fast: harvest everything, save seeds.
🥬
ALL SEASONS CABBAGE
Brassica oleracea
GREENHOUSE Aug–Sept start 75–95 days
HOW TO START
Start indoors in greenhouse August–September in cell trays. Thin to 1 per cell. Transplant outdoors October–November at 18 inch spacing. Heavy feeder — amend bed with compost before planting.
GROWING NOTES
Water consistently — irregular watering causes heads to split. Harvest when head feels firm and solid when squeezed. October–March harvest is ideal for New Orleans.
COMPANIONS
✦ Dill, mint, rosemary, thyme, nasturtiums
✕ avoid: Strawberries, tomatoes, peppers
TROUBLESHOOT
Heads split: twist plant slightly near maturity to slow growth. Caterpillar holes: cabbage loopers — check leaf undersides, hand-pick or use Bt. Yellowing leaves: nitrogen deficiency.
🫘
ARIKARA YELLOW BUSH BEAN
Phaseolus vulgaris
DIRECT SOW Mar–May, Aug–Sept 60–65 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow only — beans hate transplanting. Sow 4–6 inches apart, 1–1.5 inches deep. Do not pre-soak. Inoculate seeds with rhizobium for maximum nitrogen fixation. Two seasons: spring and fall.
GROWING NOTES
Native American heirloom — exceptional drought and heat tolerance. Nitrogen fixer: after harvest, chop plants into bed as green manure.
COMPANIONS
✦ Squash, corn, basil, marigolds
✕ avoid: Onions, fennel, garlic
TROUBLESHOOT
Pods tough: harvested too late — pick every 2 days. No pods: too much nitrogen from other legumes — don't fertilize. Rust spots: improve airflow, avoid overhead watering.
🎃
DELICATA SQUASH
Cucurbita pepo
DIRECT SOW Late July 80–100 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow late July at arch base alongside Cinderella pumpkin. Sow 2 seeds, thin to strongest. Train up arch — small fruit (1–2 lbs) doesn't need slings like the Cinderella.
GROWING NOTES
Cream skin with green stripes. Edible skin — no peeling needed! Harvest when fully cream/tan with dried corky stem. Stores months at room temp.
COMPANIONS
✦ Beans, corn, nasturtiums, marigolds
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Powdery mildew: almost inevitable in Louisiana — manageable, plant usually still fruits. Blossom drop: hand-pollinate morning female flowers. Vine borer: sawdust at stem base — inject Bt.
🌽
AMBROSIA SWEET CORN
Zea mays
DIRECT SOW Feb–Mar, Sept 75 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow in 4×4 BLOCK minimum — corn is wind-pollinated and rows alone give poor ear fill. Space plants 10–12 inches. Sow 2 per spot, thin to 1. Soil must be 60°F+.
GROWING NOTES
Hand-pollinate by shaking tassels over silks each morning. Eat within hours of harvest — sweetness fades fast. Drop mineral oil on silk tips when silks emerge to deter earworms.
COMPANIONS
✦ Beans, squash, borage, sunflowers
✕ avoid: Tomatoes — competing heavy feeders
TROUBLESHOOT
Poorly filled ears: poor pollination — hand-pollinate daily. Earworms: mineral oil on silks. Smut (gray galls): remove and bag, don't compost.
🥒
BOSTON PICKLING CUCUMBER
Cucumis sativus
DIRECT SOW Late Jan–Feb / March 55–65 days
HOW TO START
Start in biodegradable pots 3–4 weeks before transplant, or direct sow in March when soil warms. Train immediately onto arch trellis. Already proven in your garden!
GROWING NOTES
Pick at 3–4 inches for pickling, daily. Check inside arch tunnel — cucumbers hide up there. Pair with bouquet dill for fresh pickles.
COMPANIONS
✦ Dill, borage, nasturtiums, radishes
✕ avoid: Sage, strong herbs
TROUBLESHOOT
Bitter cucumbers: heat stress or irregular watering. Sudden wilt: cucumber beetle spreading bacterial wilt — pull affected plants immediately.
🍅
CHOCOLATE STRIPES TOMATO
Solanum lycopersicum
GREENHOUSE Dec–Jan 75–85 days
HOW TO START
Start in greenhouse December–January. Bury stem DEEP when transplanting — tomatoes root along buried stem. Pinch suckers weekly. Train up arch, tying weekly.
GROWING NOTES
Beautiful dark red/brown 8–12 oz fruit with green stripes. Never refrigerate. Harvest at first color change and ripen on counter.
COMPANIONS
✦ Basil, borage, marigolds, parsley, chives
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas, corn
TROUBLESHOOT
Blossom drop: heat above 90°F — normal, resumes in September. Blossom end rot: eggshells in compost tea + consistent watering. Cracking: sudden rain after dry spell — mulch well.
🍅
MATT'S WILD CHERRY TOMATO
Solanum lycopersicum
GREENHOUSE Dec–Jan 60–65 days
HOW TO START
Start greenhouse December–January. Most heat-tolerant tomato in your collection — ancestrally from hot Mexico. May keep producing through July when others stop. Very vigorous — give its own pot early.
GROWING NOTES
Hundreds of tiny intensely-flavored fruits. Self-seeds prolifically — you may get volunteers for years. Eat straight off the vine.
COMPANIONS
✦ Basil, borage, marigolds, parsley
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Cracking: inconsistent watering — harvest frequently. Overwhelming quantity: pinch some growing tips to focus energy.
🫑
JIMMY NARDELLO PEPPER
Capsicum annuum
GREENHOUSE Dec–Jan 80–90 days
HOW TO START
Start greenhouse December–January on heat mat (80–85°F minimum). 14–21 day germination. Harden off 2 weeks before March outdoor planting. Perennial in 9b — cut back by 2/3 in February!
GROWING NOTES
Long, thin, bright red sweet pepper — 0 Scoville. One of the world's most prized frying peppers. Year 2 plants are dramatically more productive.
COMPANIONS
✦ Basil, carrots, tomatoes, marigolds
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Flower drop: temp extremes — normal, will resume. Slow germination: soil too cool — 80°F minimum, no exceptions. Purple leaves: phosphorus deficiency in cold soil — wait for warmth.
🌶️
JALAPEÑO
Capsicum annuum
GREENHOUSE Dec–Jan 70–85 days
HOW TO START
Start greenhouse December–January on heat mat. Same requirements as all peppers. Perennial in 9b — cut back in February for bigger year 2 plant. Smoke red jalapeños over your pecan wood for homemade chipotles!
GROWING NOTES
Cork lines (stretch marks on skin) = mature and at peak heat. Harvest green for classic heat or let ripen red for sweeter, slightly less hot fruit.
COMPANIONS
✦ Basil, carrots, tomatoes, marigolds
✕ avoid: Fennel, apricot
TROUBLESHOOT
Not hot enough: more sun and slight water stress increases capsaicin. Pepper weevils: holes in fruit — remove and trash, never compost.
🌶️
SWEET BANANA PEPPER
Capsicum annuum
GREENHOUSE Dec–Jan 70–75 days
HOW TO START
Start greenhouse December–January alongside other peppers — identical requirements. Perennial in 9b. One plant yields dozens of 6–8 inch fruits. Great for pickling — the surplus is extraordinary pickled.
GROWING NOTES
Harvest yellow-green through orange-red — each stage has different sweetness. Red = sweetest. Heaviest harvest usually September–October as temps moderate.
COMPANIONS
✦ Basil, carrots, tomatoes, marigolds
✕ avoid: Fennel, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Same as other peppers. Anthracnose (soft spots): improve airflow, harvest promptly, avoid overhead watering.
🎃
CINDERELLA PUMPKIN
Cucurbita maxima
DIRECT SOW Late July 100–110 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow at arch base late July. Plant 2–3 seeds 1 inch deep, thin to 1 strongest vine per arch side. Train onto arch immediately — vine moves fast in the first week.
GROWING NOTES
Fruits reach 15–25 lbs — MUST make pantyhose slings tied to arch when fruit is tennis-ball size. Each vine produces 2–3 fruits. Flesh is culinary-grade: extraordinary for soup and pie.
COMPANIONS
✦ Beans, corn, nasturtiums, marigolds
✕ avoid: Brassicas, fennel
TROUBLESHOOT
Fruit drops before maturity: no sling support — always sling by tennis-ball size. Poor fruit set: hand-pollinate female flowers (tiny pumpkin at base) with brush.
🥜
VIRGINIA PEANUT
Arachis hypogaea
DIRECT SOW April–May 120–130 days
HOW TO START
Plant raw (NOT roasted!) shelled peanuts 2 inches deep, 6 inches apart. Soil must be 65°F+. Do NOT fertilize with nitrogen. Do NOT disturb soil once pegging begins — the plant shoots pegs down into soil to form pods.
GROWING NOTES
Already growing in your garden! Harvest when leaves yellow. Cure upside down in dry ventilated spot 2–4 weeks before eating. Boil some green-stage for traditional Louisiana boiled peanuts!
COMPANIONS
✦ Sweet potatoes, squash, sunflowers
✕ avoid: Onions, garlic
TROUBLESHOOT
Empty shells: poor soil contact during pegging. Mold on shells: cure in better-ventilated spot. Leaf spots: common in Louisiana, rarely fatal.
🐦
BIRDHOUSE GOURD
Lagenaria siceraria
DIRECT SOW March–April 110–130 days
HOW TO START
Soak seeds 24 hours + nick seed coat with nail file before planting. Start in biodegradable pots or direct sow April. White flowers open ONLY at night — hand-pollinate at dusk with a brush.
GROWING NOTES
Very vigorous — can climb 15–20 feet, covers entire arch and then some. Fruit dries ON the vine into hard shells. Purple martins love these as birdhouses — Louisiana tradition!
COMPANIONS
✦ Corn, beans, nasturtiums
✕ avoid: Fennel, potatoes
TROUBLESHOOT
Poor germination: seed coat not softened — always soak 24 hrs + nick. No fruit set: hand-pollinate at dusk only. Mold on drying gourds: wipe with dilute bleach, dry in sun.
🥒
ITALIAN STRIPED ZUCCHINI
Cucurbita pepo
DIRECT SOW Mar–Apr, Aug–Sept 50–55 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow in-ground BESIDE beds — not inside, it spreads 3–4 ft. Plant 2 seeds, thin to 1. More heat-tolerant than standard zucchini — better suited for New Orleans.
GROWING NOTES
Better flavor than standard zucchini — nuttier, firmer. Harvest at 6–8 inches. Male flowers (no tiny zucchini behind) can be harvested and stuffed or fried. Hand-pollinate female flowers.
COMPANIONS
✦ Beans, corn, nasturtiums, marigolds, dill
✕ avoid: Fennel, potatoes, brassicas
TROUBLESHOOT
Blossom end rot: incomplete pollination — hand-pollinate every female flower. Squash vine borer: most common killer in NOLA — sawdust at stem base, inject Bt, bury stem to re-root.
🌿
BORAGE
Borago officinalis
DIRECT SOW Feb–Apr, Sept–Oct 50–60 days
HOW TO START
ALWAYS direct sow — taproot resents transplanting. Fall-sown borage often overwinters in New Orleans. Plant among tomatoes — repels hornworm and attracts pollinators.
GROWING NOTES
Blue star-shaped flowers are edible — freeze in ice cubes for cocktails! Dynamic accumulator: leaves rich in minerals for compost tea. Bees swarm it.
COMPANIONS
✦ Tomatoes, squash, strawberries
✕ avoid: None — universal companion
TROUBLESHOOT
Aphid magnet: this is intended — it acts as a trap crop keeping aphids off tomatoes. Sprawling: stake with one cane. Not flowering: needs more sun.
🌺
BEE BALM
Monarda didyma/fistulosa
GREENHOUSE Jan–Feb Blooms yr 2
HOW TO START
COLD STRATIFY FIRST: damp paper towel in fridge 2 weeks before sowing. Then surface sow in greenhouse — needs light to germinate. 10–40 days germination (slow — be patient!).
GROWING NOTES
Native Louisiana wildflower. Perennial — comes back bigger every year. Hummingbirds and bumblebees intensely attracted. Leaves make a lovely tea. Perfect under your pecan tree.
COMPANIONS
✦ Tomatoes, peppers, eggplant
✕ avoid: None known
TROUBLESHOOT
Powdery mildew: very common — choose M. fistulosa (more resistant), improve airflow. Slow first year: completely normal. Spreading aggressively: divide clumps in spring.
🌿
SPEARMINT
Mentha spicata
GREENHOUSE Jan–Feb Year-round
HOW TO START
CONTAIN IN A POT — invasive! Mint spreads aggressively by underground runners and will take over beds within one season if planted freely. Start from seed or root a cutting in water (roots in 1 week).
GROWING NOTES
Classic culinary mint — mojitos, juleps, tea, cooking. Thrives in partial shade under your pecan. Deters aphids and spider mites from nearby plants through aromatic oils.
COMPANIONS
✦ Tomatoes, peppers, brassicas
✕ avoid: Plant in container — never in ground
TROUBLESHOOT
Taking over everything: back in container immediately. Rust spots (orange pustules): mint rust fungus — pull and bag, never compost. Root rot: too wet — needs drainage.
🌸
MAMMOTH BASIL
Ocimum basilicum
GREENHOUSE Feb–Mar 60–75 days
HOW TO START
Start greenhouse February–March, 6–8 weeks before outdoor transplant. Sow on surface, barely cover. Pinch seedlings early for bushy growth. 4–5 inch leaves spectacular for whole-leaf Caprese.
GROWING NOTES
Pinch flower buds THE MOMENT they appear — once it flowers leaves become bitter. Succession start new pot every 6–8 weeks April through September for continuous supply.
COMPANIONS
✦ Tomatoes, peppers, asparagus
✕ avoid: Sage, common rue
TROUBLESHOOT
Black spots on leaves: overhead watering — water soil level only. Bitter flavor: flowers not pinched — cut plant back by half, new growth will be sweet.
🌸
PINK SWISS CHARD
Beta vulgaris
DIRECT SOW Sept–Mar 50–60 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow or start indoors. Each seed is a cluster of 2–3 seeds — thin to 1 plant per spot, 6–8 inches apart. Cut outer leaves only; never harvest more than 1/3 at once.
GROWING NOTES
Hot pink stems with glossy dark green leaves — genuinely ornamental. More heat-tolerant than lettuce or spinach. Use pink stems in floral arrangements! Nearly year-round harvest potential.
COMPANIONS
✦ Beans, onions, brassicas, lettuce
✕ avoid: None significant
TROUBLESHOOT
Bolting: heat — harvest everything, resow September. Leaf miners (white trails): very common, remove affected leaves. Slugs after rain: crushed eggshells around base.
💙
BACHELOR BUTTONS
Centaurea cyanus
DIRECT SOW Oct–Jan 60–90 days
HOW TO START
Direct sow ONLY outdoors October–January — needs cool temps to germinate. Just scatter seeds and rake in lightly. Do NOT start indoors. Self-seeds freely, returning each cool season.
GROWING NOTES
Electric blue, pink, white, purple. Edible flowers. Blooms February–April. Attracts beneficial insects in late winter when pollinators need food most. Deadhead to prolong blooming.
COMPANIONS
✦ Tomatoes, squash, carrots
✕ avoid: None
TROUBLESHOOT
No germination: soil too warm — must be below 65°F. Aphids on buds: blast with water, bees arrive to eat them. Stops blooming: heat — let go to seed for next year.
🥒
ASHLEY CUCUMBER
Cucumis sativus
DIRECT SOW Late Jan / March 65 days
HOW TO START
Start in greenhouse late January in biodegradable pots for March transplant, OR direct sow March when soil warms. Plant pot and all. Train immediately onto arch with loose ties. Already proven in your garden!
GROWING NOTES
Developed at Clemson University specifically for the hot, humid Southeast. Pick at 6–7 inches DAILY — unharvested fruits signal DONE to the plant and slow production. Check inside arch.
COMPANIONS
✦ Dill, borage, nasturtiums, radishes
✕ avoid: Sage, strong herbs
TROUBLESHOOT
Bitter fruit: inconsistent watering — #1 cause. Slow production: unharvested fruits. More disease-resistant than Boston Pickling — better choice if you experience disease pressure.

Your pecan tree has been building a mycorrhizal fungal network in your soil for years. Your raised beds are likely tapping into it. To protect and strengthen it: never use fungicides anywhere in the yard. Add mycorrhizal inoculant to transplant holes. Your no-dig, living mulch philosophy is already perfect. Plant native species around the tree base (goldenrod, spiderwort, wild bergamot) to strengthen the network.

👃
FASTEST ID METHOD: THE SMELL TEST
Crush a leaf and smell it. Lemon/citrus = lemon balm (keep). Strong mint = wild mint (keep). Herbal/catnip = catnip (keep). No real smell = likely chamber bitter or caesar weed (pull or use for tea). Milky sap = spurge (pull and wash hands).
